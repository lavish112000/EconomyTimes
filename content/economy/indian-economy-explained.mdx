### 1.2 GDP Measurement Methods

India's GDP is calculated using three approaches:

1. **Production Approach**: Sum of value added across all sectors
2. **Expenditure Approach**: C + I + G + (X - M)
   - C = Private Consumption (58% of GDP)
   - I = Investment (32%)
   - G = Government Spending (11%)
   - X-M = Net Exports (-1%, trade deficit)
3. **Income Approach**: Sum of all factor incomes

## 2. Inflation in India: Types, Causes, and Control

### 2.1 Inflation Measurement

India uses two primary inflation indices:

**Consumer Price Index (CPI)**
- Measures retail price changes for households
- RBI's primary inflation targeting metric
- Target: 4% Â± 2% (2-6% tolerance band)
- Components: Food (46%), Fuel (7%), Core (47%)

**Wholesale Price Index (WPI)**
- Measures wholesale price changes
- Used for business contracts and tax calculations
- More volatile than CPI

<Callout type="info">
**RBI Inflation Targeting Framework**: Since 2016, RBI has a legal mandate to maintain CPI inflation at 4% with a tolerance band of Â±2%. If inflation stays outside this range for 3 consecutive quarters, RBI must explain the failure to the government.
</Callout>

1. **Monsoon Dependency**: 42% of workforce in agriculture; poor monsoons spike food prices
2. **Oil Import Vulnerability**: 80% oil imports make India sensitive to global crude prices
3. **Structural Issues**: Poor storage, distribution inefficiencies increase food inflation

### 2.3 RBI's Monetary Policy Tools

The Reserve Bank of India controls inflation through:

| Tool | Current Rate (Jan 2025) | Impact |
|------|------------------------|--------|
| **Repo Rate** | 6.50% | Primary lending rate to banks |
| **Reverse Repo Rate** | 3.35% | Rate at which RBI borrows from banks |
| **CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio)** | 4.50% | Cash banks must hold with RBI |
| **SLR (Statutory Liquidity Ratio)** | 18.00% | Liquid assets banks must maintain |

<Callout type="warning">
**Interest Rate Sensitivity**: A 25 basis point (0.25%) repo rate hike typically takes 6-9 months to fully impact inflation through the monetary transmission mechanism. This lag makes forecasting and timely action critical for RBI.
</Callout>

## Frequently Asked Questions

<details>
<summary>**What is the difference between GDP and GVA?**</summary>

**GDP (Gross Domestic Product)** measures the total value of goods and services produced in India, including net product taxes (taxes minus subsidies).

**GVA (Gross Value Added)** measures the value of goods and services produced before adjusting for taxes and subsidies.

**Relationship**: GDP = GVA + (Product Taxes - Subsidies)

For exam purposes, GVA is considered a better measure of sectoral performance as it's not affected by tax changes.
</details>

<details>
<summary>**Why is India's inflation higher than developed countries?**</summary>

India's higher inflation is due to:
1. **Food weight**: 46% CPI weight vs 15-20% in developed nations
2. **Supply-side constraints**: Poor storage, monsoon dependency
3. **Energy imports**: 80% oil import dependency makes India vulnerable to global price shocks
4. **Growing demand**: Rising incomes increase consumption faster than supply
5. **Currency depreciation**: Rupee depreciation makes imports costlier

RBI targets 4% inflation, higher than US Fed's 2% target, acknowledging these structural factors.
</details>

<details>

RBI uses multiple tools:

**Primary Tools:**
- **Repo Rate**: Increase to make borrowing costlier â†’ reduces demand â†’ lowers inflation

**Transmission Mechanism**: RBI action â†’ Bank lending rates â†’ Consumer borrowing â†’ Demand â†’ Prices (takes 6-9 months)

<details>
<summary>**Is India really becoming the 3rd largest economy?**</summary>

**Current Status** (2024): India is the 5th largest economy at $3.7 trillion nominal GDP.

**Projections for 2027-2030**:
- Overtake Germany (~$4.1T) by 2026-27
- Overtake Japan (~$4.2T) by 2027-28
- Become 3rd largest by 2030

**Required Conditions:**
1. Sustained 7-8% GDP growth
2. Favorable exchange rate (rupee shouldn't depreciate rapidly)
3. No major global recessions
4. Continued reforms and investment

**Reality Check**: India will be 3rd by total GDP, but per capita income will still be around $4,000-5,000, placing it in lower-middle-income category. China crossed $10,000 per capita in 2019.
</details>

<details>
<summary>**What is fiscal deficit and why does it matter?**</summary>

**Fiscal Deficit** = Total Government Expenditure - Total Government Revenue (excluding borrowings)

**India's Target**: 5.6% of GDP (FY 2024-25), aiming for 4.5% by 2025-26

**Why It Matters:**
1. **Borrowing**: Higher deficit means more government borrowing â†’ crowding out private investment
2. **Debt**: Persistent deficits increase debt-to-GDP ratio (currently 82%)
3. **Interest Burden**: 20% of government spending goes to debt servicing
4. **Inflation**: Excessive deficit can be inflationary if financed by printing money

**FRBM Act** (Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management) mandates deficit reduction path.
</details>

<summary>**How does India's economy compare to China's?**</summary>

**Size Comparison:**
- China GDP: $18.2 trillion (5x India's $3.7T)

- China growth: 5.0% vs India: 7.2%

**India's Advantages:**
- Younger population (median age 28 vs China's 39)
- Democratic system attracting Western companies ("China+1")
- English-speaking workforce
- Vibrant entrepreneurial ecosystem
- Stable political environment

**China's Advantages:**
- 30-year head start in economic reforms
- Superior infrastructure
- Manufacturing prowess (30% global manufacturing vs India's 3%)
- Higher savings and investment rate

**Timeline**: At current growth rates, India would reach China's current GDP level in approximately 20-25 years, but by then China would have grown further.
</details>
